法庭意见书要素 (Case Brief Elements)

Case Citation

Korematsu v. United States, 323 U.S. 214 (1944)

Parties

Petitioner: Fred Korematsu
Respondent: United States

Procedural History

Convicted in federal district court, affirmed by Circuit Court of Appeals, certiorari granted by Supreme Court.

Facts

Korematsu, an American citizen of Japanese descent, remained in a military exclusion zone contrary to Civilian Exclusion Order No. 34.

Issue

Whether the exclusion order violated Korematsu's constitutional rights.

Holding

The Supreme Court upheld the conviction, finding the exclusion order constitutional as a wartime measure.

Reasoning

The Court deferred to military judgment regarding necessity, distinguishing between racial prejudice and military necessity.

Dissenting Opinions

Justices Roberts, Murphy, and Jackson filed dissenting opinions criticizing the decision as racial discrimination.

Majority Opinion Excerpt

"It should be noted, to begin with, that all legal restrictions which curtail the civil rights of a single racial group are immediately suspect. That is not to say that all such restrictions are unconstitutional. It is to say that courts must subject them to the most rigid scrutiny. Pressing public necessity may sometimes justify the existence of such restrictions; racial antagonism never can."

— Justice Black, delivering the opinion of the Court

Dissenting Opinion Excerpt

"I dissent, because I think the indisputable facts exhibit a clear violation of Constitutional rights. This is not a case of keeping people off the streets at night... it is the case of convicting a citizen as a punishment for not submitting to imprisonment in a concentration camp, based on his ancestry, and solely because of his ancestry..."

— Justice Roberts, dissenting
